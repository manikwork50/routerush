Most Visited Places in India - North India

Taj Mahal — Agra, Uttar Pradesh

History

Commissioned by Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in 1632 and substantially completed by 1648 (with finishing works continuing into the 1650s), the Taj Mahal is a mausoleum built for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal. It stands on the right bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, the then Mughal capital. The design fuses Persian, Timurid, and Indo-Islamic elements into a perfect, symmetrical composition: a white marble tomb raised on a square plinth, framed by four minarets, preceded by a grand gate (darwaza-i rauza), and set within a charbagh (four-quartered) garden aligned on a central axis. Calligraphy inlaid with black marble, pietra dura floral panels using semi-precious stones (jasper, jade, lapis, carnelian), and exquisitely carved jali screens create an effect of weightless intricacy. Over centuries, the Taj has survived political change, colonial curiosity, and environmental challenges, yet retains its emotional pull: a monument to memory, grief, and imperial craftsmanship at its pinnacle.

Why It’s Famous

Beyond its love-story aura, the Taj is renowned as perhaps the most perfectly proportioned building in the world. The way its white Makrana marble changes tone through the day—rosy at dawn, luminous at noon, golden at sunset—adds drama to the symmetry. It’s a UNESCO World Heritage Site and among the “Seven Wonders” in popular lists, drawing millions of visitors annually. For many travelers, the first glimpse through the gate—where the tomb appears framed like a living painting—is unforgettable.

Where It Is

Agra, in western Uttar Pradesh, about 230–240 km southeast of New Delhi, on the Yamuna River.

How to Reach

From Delhi, take the Gatimaan or Vande Bharat Express (under 2 hours) to Agra. NH-19 via the Yamuna Expressway (about 3.5–4 hours by car) is the fastest road link. From Jaipur or Gwalior/Jhansi, direct trains and highways connect easily.

Approx. Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest Bus Stand: Idgah Bus Stand, Agra — ~6 km
Nearest Railway Stations: Agra Fort — ~3 km; Agra Cantt — ~6 km
Nearest Airport: Agra (Kheria) Airport — ~12 km; New Delhi (DEL) — ~225 km

Who Built It

Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal as a mausoleum for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal.

Golden Temple — Amritsar, Punjab

History

The Golden Temple, also known as Harmandir Sahib or Darbar Sahib, is the holiest shrine in Sikhism. It was founded by the fourth Sikh Guru, Guru Ram Das, in 1577, and the construction was completed by his successor Guru Arjan Dev in 1604. The temple was rebuilt and covered with gold foil in the early 19th century by Maharaja Ranjit Singh, which gave it its iconic golden appearance. The temple complex has survived invasions and attacks throughout history and remains a symbol of Sikh spirituality, peace, and resilience.

Why It’s Famous

Renowned for its magnificent golden façade and the serene Amrit Sarovar (holy pool) that surrounds it, the Golden Temple attracts millions of visitors and pilgrims annually. It is celebrated for its inclusiveness, welcoming people from all faiths and backgrounds. The temple’s continuous langar (community kitchen) serves free meals to tens of thousands of people every day — embodying Sikh values of charity and equality.

Where It Is

Located in the city of Amritsar in Punjab, India, near the India-Pakistan border.

How to Reach

Amritsar is well-connected by road, rail, and air. The Golden Temple is easily accessible from Amritsar’s city center.

Approx. Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest Bus Stand: Amritsar ISBT — ~3 km
Nearest Railway Station: Amritsar Junction (Amritsar Cantt) — ~2 km
Nearest Airport: Sri Guru Ram Dass Jee International Airport — ~15 km

Who Built It

Originally founded by Guru Ram Das, with significant contributions and renovation by Guru Arjan Dev and Maharaja Ranjit Singh.

India Gate — New Delhi, Delhi

History

India Gate is a war memorial located in the heart of New Delhi, designed by Sir Edwin Lutyens. Completed in 1931, it commemorates the 70,000 Indian soldiers who lost their lives fighting for the British Indian Army during World War I and the Third Anglo-Afghan War. The structure is inspired by the Arc de Triomphe in Paris and stands as an imposing archway on Rajpath, forming a significant part of Delhi's ceremonial corridor.

Why It’s Famous

India Gate is a popular spot for locals and tourists alike, offering a mix of history and leisure. The monument is illuminated beautifully at night, and its expansive lawns are a favored picnic spot. The Amar Jawan Jyoti, or the Flame of the Immortal Soldier, was added later to honor soldiers who died in 1971 Indo-Pakistan war.

Where It Is

Located on Rajpath, central New Delhi, near Connaught Place and Rashtrapati Bhavan.

How to Reach

Easily accessible by Delhi Metro—the Central Secretariat and Patel Chowk stations are nearest. Several bus routes service the area, and it is connected by wide roads for taxis and personal vehicles.

Approx. Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest Metro Stations: Central Secretariat (1 km), Patel Chowk (1.5 km)
Nearest Bus Stop: India Gate Bus Stop (adjacent)
Nearest Airport: Indira Gandhi International Airport (20 km)

Who Built It

Designed by British architect Sir Edwin Lutyens as part of his plan for New Delhi.

Hawa Mahal — Jaipur, Rajasthan

History

The Hawa Mahal or "Palace of Winds" was built in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh, designed by Lal Chand Ustad. It was constructed as an extension of the City Palace to allow royal ladies to observe street festivals unseen by the public, adhering to the purdah tradition. The five-storey pink sandstone structure is a striking example of Rajput architecture influenced by Mughal style, featuring 953 small windows ("jharokhas") with intricate latticework.

Why It’s Famous

The Mahal is famous for its unique honeycomb façade and sheer architectural beauty. Its latticework offers cool breeze throughout the year—hence the name "Palace of Winds"—and provides excellent ventilation. It is one of Jaipur’s most iconic landmarks and a popular photography spot, especially during sunrise and sunset.

Where It Is

Located in the heart of Jaipur city, Rajasthan, near the City Palace.

How to Reach

Jaipur is well connected by road, rail, and air. The Hawa Mahal is easily accessible from Jaipur Railway Station (3 km) and Jaipur International Airport (15 km). Local buses, auto-rickshaws, and taxis are readily available for the short trip from the station/airport.

Approx. Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest Bus Station: Sindhi Camp Bus Stand, Jaipur — ~2.5 km
Nearest Railway Station: Jaipur Junction — ~3 km
Nearest Airport: Jaipur International Airport — ~15 km

Who Built It

Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh commissioned the Hawa Mahal, designed by architect Lal Chand Ustad.

Varanasi Ghats — Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

History

Varanasi, also known as Kashi or Banaras, is one of the oldest inhabited cities in the world, with history dating back over 3000 years. The ghats are riverfront steps leading down to the banks of the Ganges River, constructed over centuries by various rulers and devotees. Varanasi has been a cultural and spiritual center, especially in Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism. The ghats host rituals, ceremonies, and festivals, continuing ancient traditions.

Why It’s Famous

Varanasi Ghats are famous for their spiritual ambiance, cremation ceremonies at Manikarnika Ghat, and the spectacular Ganga Aarti ritual performed every evening. The city attracts pilgrims and tourists worldwide seeking spiritual enlightenment, cultural immersion, and history.

Where It Is

Situated on the banks of the Ganges River in Varanasi, Eastern Uttar Pradesh, India.

How to Reach

Varanasi is accessible by Lal Bahadur Shastri International Airport (~26 km from city), Varanasi Junction (by rail), and regular bus networks from major cities. The ghats are located centrally along the riverside, reachable by local rickshaws and taxis.

Approx. Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest Bus Station: Varanasi Central Bus Stand — ~4 km
Nearest Railway Station: Varanasi Junction — ~6 km
Nearest Airport: Lal Bahadur Shastri International Airport — ~26 km

Who Built It

The ghats were developed over centuries by various rulers including the Marathas and Mughal emperors, with key contributions from local patrons and sadhus.

Varanasi Ghats (Dashashwamedh–Manikarnika belt) — Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

History

Varanasi (Kashi/Benares) is amongst the world’s oldest living cities. Myth credits Lord Shiva with founding it; historical references stretch back over two millennia. Its riverfront ghats—stone steps descending to the Ganges—were extensively rebuilt by Maratha rulers in the 18th–19th centuries, bundling palaces, temples, and dharamshalas into a continuous architectural theater. Dashashwamedh Ghat hosts elaborate evening aarti ceremonies, while Manikarnika and Harishchandra Ghats are principal cremation grounds central to Hindu beliefs about life, death, and moksha (liberation). The city’s labyrinthine lanes shelter shrines, akharas, silk weavers, and classical music traditions.

Why It’s Famous

Varanasi distills India’s spiritual intensity: sunrise boat rides with saffron-clad priests, chanting students, and pilgrims bathing; the hypnotic aarti’s lamps mirrored in the river at dusk; and the frank proximity to mortality at the cremation ghats. It’s also famed for Banarasi silk sarees, paan, thandai, and a fiercely alive street culture. Walking from Assi to Manikarnika, absorbing scents of incense and marigold, temple bells, and flute strains, is a profound experience.

Where It Is

On the left bank of the Ganges in southeastern Uttar Pradesh, approximately 320 km east of Lucknow and 220 km southeast of Prayagraj (Allahabad).

How to Reach

Excellent rail connectivity from Delhi, Kolkata, Mumbai, and Lucknow. Flights to Lal Bahadur Shastri International Airport near Babatpur and good highways connect via NH19/NH31.

Approximate Distances from Transit Hubs (to Dashashwamedh Ghat)

Nearest Bus Stand: Varanasi Cantt/City Bus Stand — ~5–6 km
Nearest Railway Station: Varanasi Junction (BSB) — ~5 km; Kashi/Varanasi City (BCY) — ~4–5 km
Nearest Airport: Lal Bahadur Shastri International Airport (VNS) — ~25 km

Who Built It

The ghats and surrounding structures were developed over centuries by rulers including the Marathas, Mughal patrons, and local devotees.

Hampi (Vijayanagara Ruins) — Ballari District, Karnataka

History

Hampi was the glittering capital of the Vijayanagara Empire (14th–16th centuries), once one of the world’s largest cities. Travelers like Niccolò de’ Conti and Domingo Paes wrote of bustling bazaars, grand processional avenues, and sophisticated water-management systems. In 1565, after the Battle of Talikota, the city was sacked, leaving a surreal landscape of granite boulders and hundreds of monuments: the Virupaksha Temple (still active), Vittala Temple with its famed stone chariot and musical pillars, royal enclosures with the Mahanavami Dibba and stepped tank, and the Lotus Mahal and Elephant Stables in the Zenana enclosure. The Tungabhadra River threads the site, and Anjanadri Hill across the river is associated with Hanuman lore.

Why It’s Famous

Few places match Hampi’s combination of archaeological scale and natural drama. You can clamber up Matanga Hill for sunrise, cycle through temple avenues, and watch langurs pose among colonnades. The craftsmanship—intricate bas-reliefs of dancers, deities, and processions; corbelled mantapas; and still-functioning water channels—makes the ruins feel surprisingly alive.

Where It Is

In central-northern Karnataka, near Hospet (Hosapete), about 340 km from Bengaluru and approximately 380 km from Hyderabad.

How to Reach

Rail travelers typically arrive at Hosapete Junction, then continue by taxi, auto, or bus to Hampi. Buses link from major Karnataka cities. The closest commercial airport with frequent service is Hubballi (HBX); a closer limited-service option is Vidyanagar (JSW) near Toranagallu, which occasionally operates scheduled flights.

Approximate Distances from Transit Hubs (to Hampi Bazaar/Virupaksha Temple area)

Nearest Bus Stand: Hospet KSRTC Bus Stand — ~13 km
Nearest Railway Station: Hosapete (Hospet) Junction — ~13 km
Nearest Airports: Vidyanagar (JSW) — ~40 km (limited schedules); Hubballi (HBX) — ~160 km; Bengaluru (BLR) — ~350 km

Who Built It

The Vijayanagara Empire, founded by Harihara I and Bukka Raya I, was responsible for constructing the city and its monumental temples and palaces.

Konark Sun Temple — Konark, Odisha

History

Built in the 13th century under Eastern Ganga king Narasimhadeva I (circa 1250 CE), the Konark Sun Temple is conceived as a colossal stone chariot for Surya, the Sun god, drawn by seven horses and set upon twenty-four intricately carved wheels. Much of the sanctum’s upper structure has collapsed over time, but the jagamohana (assembly hall) and surviving sculptures remain among India’s finest temple carvings: celestial musicians, dancers frozen mid-gesture, mythical beasts, and narrative friezes that reveal courtly life, maritime trade, erotica, and devotional scenes. Konark, along with Puri Jagannath and Bhubaneswar’s Lingaraja, forms Odisha’s sacred triangle and exemplifies Kalinga temple architecture—curvilinear shikharas, ornate doorjambs, and precise iconographic programs.

Why It’s Famous

The sheer scale and ambition of turning a temple into a moving cosmic diagram is breathtaking. The stone wheels double as sundials, and the horses seem to surge forward from the platform. Konark’s carvings are both technically superb and humanly tender, making it a dream for historians, photographers, and anyone interested in the evolution of Indian temple art. The annual Konark Dance Festival celebrates classical forms against this dramatic backdrop.

Where It Is

On Odisha’s coast, near the Bay of Bengal, about 35 km northeast of Puri and approximately 65 km southeast of Bhubaneswar.

How to Reach

Travelers often base themselves in Puri or Bhubaneswar. Regular buses and taxis link both cities to Konark via the scenic Marine Drive Road from Puri or NH316 from Bhubaneswar.

Approximate Distances from Transit Hubs

Nearest bus stands: Puri Bus Stand — ~35 km; Bhubaneswar Baramunda Bus Stand — ~65 km
Nearest railway stations: Puri — ~35 km; Bhubaneswar — ~65 km
Nearest airport: Biju Patnaik International Airport (BBI), Bhubaneswar — ~65 km

Who Built It

Eastern Ganga king Narasimhadeva I commissioned the temple, completed by skilled artisans of Kalinga architecture.

Jaipur City Palace & Amber Fort — Jaipur, Rajasthan

History

Founded in 1727 by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II, Jaipur was India’s first planned city, designed according to Vastu Shastra and Shilpa Shastra principles. The City Palace remains the ceremonial heart, blending Rajput, Mughal, and European styles. It includes courtyards and grand gateways like the Peacock Gate, Mubarak Mahal museum, and Chandra Mahal royal residence. Nearby, Amber (Amer) Fort predates Jaipur—built by Raja Man Singh I in the late 16th century and expanded by successive Kachwaha rulers. Its sandstone and marble palaces, including Sheesh Mahal and Diwan-i-Khas, overlook Maota Lake. Amber’s strategic hilltop location made it the Kachwahas’ seat before Jaipur’s founding.

Why It’s Famous

The City Palace and Amber Fort epitomize Rajasthan’s regal past. Amber’s Sheesh Mahal dazzles with mirrors glittering in candlelight. Elephant rides, sound and light shows, and panoramic views create unforgettable experiences. The City Palace still houses Jaipur’s royals and anchors the Pink City’s bustling bazaar areas such as Johari and Bapu markets.

Where It Is

Central and northern Jaipur, Rajasthan, approximately 280 km southwest of Delhi.

How to Reach

Direct trains like the Shatabdi and Vande Bharat connect Delhi to Jaipur in 4–5 hours. Road travel is convenient via NH48 (Delhi–Jaipur Expressway), taking 5–6 hours by car. Jaipur International Airport connects to all major metros.

Approximate Distances

City Palace: Sindhi Camp Bus Stand ~3 km; Jaipur Junction ~5 km; Jaipur Airport ~13 km.
Amber Fort: Sindhi Camp ~11 km; Jaipur Junction ~13 km; Jaipur Airport ~21 km.

Who Built It

Jaipur City Palace was commissioned by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II; Amber Fort was built by Raja Man Singh I with expansions by his successors.

Ajanta & Ellora Caves — Aurangabad, Maharashtra

History

Ajanta Caves (2nd century BCE–6th century CE) are famous for rock-cut Buddhist chaityas and viharas adorned with exquisite murals depicting Jataka tales. Ellora Caves (5th–10th century CE) consist of 34 caves showcasing Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain traditions. The highlight is Cave 16 (Kailasa Temple), a monolithic structure carved top-down from basalt rock, symbolizing Mount Kailash. Patronage came from several dynasties including Vakatakas, Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas, and Yadavas.

Why It’s Famous

Ajanta’s paintings are among the world’s finest ancient Buddhist art, featuring refined shading and storytelling. Ellora’s Kailasa Temple is an engineering marvel, created by excavating 200,000 tons of rock and displaying intricate sculptures. The caves reflect India’s religious pluralism and architectural genius.

Where It Is

Near Aurangabad (now Chhatrapati Sambhajinagar), Maharashtra. Ajanta is approximately 100 km northeast; Ellora about 30 km northwest.

How to Reach

Aurangabad is connected by rail from Mumbai, Pune, and Hyderabad; by air via Aurangabad Airport; and via NH52 and NH753F roads. Taxis and buses link Aurangabad to Ajanta and Ellora.

Approximate Distances (Ellora)

Bus Stand (Aurangabad Central) ~30 km
Railway Station (Aurangabad Jn) ~30 km
Airport (Aurangabad IXU) ~36 km

Who Built It

The caves were commissioned by various ruling dynasties over centuries, reflecting Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain religious patronage.

Gateway of India & Colaba — Mumbai, Maharashtra

History

Completed in 1924 to commemorate King George V’s 1911 visit, the Gateway of India is a grand Indo-Saracenic style archway. It became symbolic as the ceremonial entrance for British viceroys and the site where British troops exited India in 1948. The Colaba waterfront evolved as a cosmopolitan hub blending colonial architecture with vibrant markets.

Why It’s Famous

The Gateway is Mumbai’s emblem overlooking the Arabian Sea. Adjacent landmarks include the iconic Taj Mahal Palace Hotel. Ferries depart nearby for the Elephanta Caves. Colaba Causeway bustles with cafés, street stalls, and galleries, where colonial grandeur meets modern India.

Where It Is

Southern tip of Mumbai, Maharashtra.

How to Reach

Local trains terminate at Churchgate/CSMT stations; taxis and buses serve Colaba. Mumbai International Airport offers global connections.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Mumbai Central) ~6 km
Railway Stations: CSMT ~2.5 km; Churchgate ~2 km
Airport (Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Intl, BOM) ~25 km

Who Built It

Built by the British colonial government during early 20th century as a ceremonial gateway.

Meenakshi Amman Temple — Madurai, Tamil Nadu

History

The Meenakshi Amman Temple was expanded to its present grandeur by Nayak ruler Tirumalai Nayaka in the 17th century. Dedicated to Goddess Meenakshi (Parvati) and Lord Sundareshwarar (Shiva), it features 14 towering gopurams richly decorated with mythological figures. The thousand-pillar hall highlights intricate Nayak architecture.

Why It’s Famous

The temple is both a pilgrimage site and architectural marvel— gopurams bursting with colorful deities and guardians. Active rituals, vibrant processions, and the renowned Chithirai Festival bring it to life annually.

Where It Is

Located in central Madurai city, Tamil Nadu, approximately 460 km southwest of Chennai.

How to Reach

Madurai Junction railway station connects across India. Madurai Airport serves domestic destinations. NH38 and NH44 highways link Madurai to Chennai, Tiruchirapalli, and Coimbatore.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Periyar Central) ~2 km
Railway Station (Madurai Junction) ~1.5 km
Airport (Madurai IXM) ~12 km

Who Built It

Developed by Nayak rulers, especially Tirumalai Nayaka, with roots in older South Indian temple traditions.

Fort Kochi & Mattancherry — Kochi, Kerala

History

Fort Kochi was influenced by Portuguese colonizers in 1503, followed by Dutch in 1663 and British in 1795. St. Francis Church, where Vasco da Gama was originally buried, is India's oldest European church. Mattancherry Palace (Dutch Palace) houses frescoes depicting the Ramayana and royal portraits. The Jewish Synagogue in Jew Town is Asia’s oldest functioning synagogue, established in 1568.

Why It’s Famous

Fort Kochi is known for its colonial-era bungalows, iconic Chinese fishing nets at sunset, spice markets, and the Kochi-Muziris Biennale, India’s largest contemporary art festival reflecting the city's unique cultural blend.

Where It Is

Located in Kochi city, central Kerala on the Arabian Sea coast.

How to Reach

Connected by Ernakulam railway stations (Junction and Town), with Cochin International Airport ~45 km away. Ferries and buses link Ernakulam with Fort Kochi.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Ernakulam KSRTC) ~12 km
Railway Station (Ernakulam Junction) ~13 km
Airport (Cochin International Airport - COK) ~45 km

Who Built It

Influenced and developed by Portuguese, Dutch, and British colonial powers between 16th and 18th centuries.

Charminar — Hyderabad, Telangana

History

Built in 1591 by Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah, founder of Hyderabad, Charminar marks the city’s foundation and commemorates the end of a plague. The square structure rises 48.7 m high, with four grand arches and four minarets capped with domes. Around it developed Hyderabad’s pulse: Laad Bazaar, famed for bangles, and Mecca Masjid, built with soil from Mecca.

Why It’s Famous

Charminar is Hyderabad’s beating heart and symbol. The evening buzz—glittering bangles, pearls, Irani chai cafés, and biryani stalls—makes it a living monument. From its balconies, the panorama of Old Hyderabad unfolds.

Where It Is

Old Hyderabad, Telangana.

How to Reach

Hyderabad’s metro and buses connect well. Secunderabad and Hyderabad Deccan railheads are close. Rajiv Gandhi International Airport has global connectivity.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (MGBS) ~3 km
Railway (Hyderabad Deccan Nampally) ~4 km; Secunderabad Jn ~9 km
Airport (HYD) ~20 km

Who Built It

Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah commissioned Charminar as part of Hyderabad’s foundation.

Goa Beaches — North & South Goa

History

Goa was under Portuguese rule from 1510 to 1961. Its coastlines developed as a fusion of Konkani traditions and Lusophone culture. In the 1960s–70s, Goa became a hippie haven, and since then its beaches—Calangute, Baga, Anjuna, Vagator (North Goa); Colva, Palolem (South Goa)—attract millions.

Why It’s Famous

Golden sands, nightlife, water sports, yoga retreats, seafood shacks, and Indo-Portuguese villages. From raves at Anjuna to quiet sunsets at Palolem, Goa offers variety unmatched in India.

Where It Is

Western India, Konkan coast, approximately 600 km south of Mumbai.

How to Reach

Vasco da Gama and Madgaon railway stations serve Goa. Goa International Airport (Dabolim) and the new Manohar International (Mopa) provide air connectivity. NH66 and buses connect from Mumbai, Pune, and Bengaluru.

Approximate Distances (to Calangute)

Railway (Thivim) ~19 km; Madgaon ~46 km
Airport (Dabolim) ~39 km; Mopa ~31 km

Who Built It

Goa’s coastal settlements evolved over centuries under local rulers and Portuguese colonial rule.

Darjeeling & Tiger Hill — West Bengal

History

Developed as a hill station by the British in the 19th century, Darjeeling became famous for its tea estates and views of Kanchenjunga. Tiger Hill (2,590 m) is renowned for sunrise panoramas where the sun lights Kanchenjunga—and even Mt. Everest on clear days. The Darjeeling Himalayan Railway (“Toy Train”) began in 1881.

Why It’s Famous

Cool climate, tea gardens, colonial schools, Buddhist monasteries, and Toy Train rides. Sunrise at Tiger Hill is legendary—thousands gather to see peaks blush with dawn.

Where It Is

Northern West Bengal, near Sikkim border.

How to Reach

NJP (New Jalpaiguri) railway is gateway. Bagdogra Airport (IXB) connects nationwide. Hill roads (NH110) lead to Darjeeling.

Approximate Distances (to Darjeeling Mall Road)

Kaziranga National Park — Assam

History

Declared a reserve forest in 1905, Kaziranga became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985. It’s world-famous as the home of the Indian one-horned rhinoceros. Its floodplains of the Brahmaputra sustain elephants, swamp deer, tigers, and migratory birds.

Why It’s Famous

The park has the world’s largest population of one-horned rhinos (~2,400). Elephant-back and jeep safaris through tall elephant grass bring visitors face-to-face with rhinos, wild buffalo, and river dolphins.

Where It Is

Golaghat & Nagaon districts, Assam, about 220 km from Guwahati.

How to Reach

Buses and taxis from Guwahati and Jorhat. Nearest railway stations are Furkating, Jorhat, or Guwahati. Nearest airports are Jorhat (97 km) and Guwahati (217 km).

Approximate Distances (Kohora Range)

Bus Stand (Kohora) ~1 km
Railway (Furkating Jn) ~75 km; Guwahati ~217 km
Airport (Jorhat JLH) ~97 km; Guwahati GAU ~217 km

Who Built It

Declared a protected forest by British Indian Government with ongoing conservation efforts.

Mysore Palace — Mysuru, Karnataka

History

Seat of the Wodeyar dynasty, Mysore Palace was rebuilt in Indo-Saracenic style by British architect Henry Irwin after a fire in 1897. Completed in 1912, it blends Hindu, Muslim, Rajput, and Gothic elements. It houses ornate halls including Durbar and Kalyana Mantapa, royal armory, and art treasures.

Why It’s Famous

Mysore Palace is India’s second most visited monument after the Taj Mahal. Its illumination on Sundays and during the Dasara festival—with 100,000 bulbs glowing—creates a fairy-tale sight. Inside, stained glass, gilded ceilings, and wall paintings dazzle visitors.

Where It Is

Mysuru, Karnataka, approximately 145 km southwest of Bengaluru.

How to Reach

Mysuru Junction railway station connects Mysuru with Bengaluru and Chennai. Mysuru Airport has regional flights. NH275 highway offers road connectivity from Bengaluru (~3–4 hours).

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Mysuru KSRTC) ~2 km
Railway (Mysuru Junction) ~3 km
Airport (Mysore MYQ) ~12 km; Bengaluru BLR ~180 km

Who Built It

The Wodeyar dynasty and British architects rebuilt the palace after the 1897 fire.

Khajuraho Temples — Madhya Pradesh

History

Built by Chandela rulers between the 9th and 11th centuries, Khajuraho originally had around 85 temples, with about 25 surviving today. The temples represent Hinduism and Jainism. The grandest is Kandariya Mahadev temple. Sculptures depict gods, apsaras, musicians, warriors, and famous erotic couples, reflecting the broader worldview of purusharthas (life’s aims).

Why It’s Famous

Khajuraho’s artistry is unparalleled—sensual yet sacred, showcasing kama (desire) as part of life. This UNESCO World Heritage site is famed for its exquisite temple carvings and the annual dance festival celebrating Indian classical dance forms.

Where It Is

Located in Chhatarpur district, Madhya Pradesh, about 175 km from Jhansi.

How to Reach

Khajuraho has its own small airport and railway station connecting via Jhansi, Satna, and Mahoba. It is accessible by roads NH39 and NH44.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Khajuraho) ~1 km
Railway Station (Khajuraho Jn) ~5 km; Satna ~120 km
Airport (Khajuraho HJR) ~5 km

Who Built It

Commissioned by the Chandela dynasty, these temples showcase their artistic and religious patronage.

Udaipur & City Palace — Rajasthan

History

Founded by Maharana Udai Singh II in 1559 after losing Chittorgarh to the Mughals, Udaipur became the capital of Mewar. The City Palace complex, begun in the 16th century and expanded over 400 years, overlooks Lake Pichola. It features white marble palaces, temples, gardens, and courtyards layered along the ridge.

Why It’s Famous

Often called the “Venice of the East,” Udaipur’s City Palace and Lake Pichola with Jag Mandir and the Lake Palace (now a luxury hotel) form postcard views. Its romance and beauty have been showcased in films and attract visitors worldwide for sunset boat rides and scenic hill scenery.

Where It Is

Located in southern Rajasthan, about 400 km southwest of Jaipur.

How to Reach

Udaipur Railway is connected to Delhi, Jaipur, and Mumbai. Maharana Pratap Airport has flights to major metros. NH48 and NH58 highways offer road connectivity.

Approximate Distances

Bus Stand (Udaipur Central) ~2 km
Railway Station (Udaipur City) ~3 km
Airport (Udaipur UDR) ~24 km

Who Built It

Founded by Maharana Udai Singh II and expanded by successive rulers of Mewar.
